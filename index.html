Mapping Social Science Discourse:
A Bibliometric Visualization of Research Trends, 1970–2054

Syed Asrar Ahmed U  ·  Independent Research  ·  asrarsaa@gmail.com

98 terms · 7 subfields · 85-year window  ·  OpenAlex Social Sciences corpus  ·  Not yet peer-reviewed

Bibliometrics Scientometrics Social Science Discourse OpenAlex D3 Treemap Logistic Projection Research Trends Knowledge Mapping
Abstract

This paper presents Social Science Word Map, an interactive bibliometric visualization tracking the rise, fall, and emergence of 98 research terms across seven social-science subfields from 1970 to 2054. Drawing on the OpenAlex open-access scholarly database — covering over 250 million works — we compute doc-frequency share, five-year compound annual growth rates (CAGR), and a novelty index for each term across 55 years of real publication data (1970–2024). A logistic extrapolation extends trajectories through 2054, modelling saturation dynamics for growing terms and asymptotic floors for declining ones.

Key findings include the dramatic acceleration of intersectionality, mental health, artificial intelligence, and decolonial discourse, alongside the measurable retreat of structuralism, cold war, and propaganda as active research vocabularies. The visualization embeds these findings in an interactive D3.js treemap with year-scrubbing, metric layers, and a click-through detail panel designed for exploratory research.

Keywords: bibliometrics · scientometrics · social science discourse · OpenAlex · treemap visualization · logistic projection · research trends · knowledge mapping

1   Introduction

The vocabulary of academic research is not static. Concepts rise into prominence, circulate widely, peak, and then recede — replaced by newer frameworks that recast old questions or open genuinely new ones. Derek de Solla Price's foundational observation that scientific literature grows exponentially[1] and Eugene Garfield's insight that citation networks encode intellectual genealogies[2] together established that the dynamics of knowledge production are themselves objects of scientific inquiry. Tracking this conceptual metabolism is not merely an exercise in academic bookkeeping; it reveals the shifting priorities, political pressures, and intellectual fashions that shape how entire disciplines understand the social world.

Bibliometrics — the quantitative study of scholarly publications — offers a principled window into this process. Bornmann and Mutz's (2015) systematic review of growth-of-science studies documents the long-run expansion of the global publication record across disciplines,[8] while Fortunato et al.'s landmark mapping of "the science of science" in Science demonstrates that bibliometric methods have matured into a rigorous empirical programme capable of quantifying creativity, collaboration, and the diffusion of ideas at population scale.[9] By treating the presence of a search term in an article's title or abstract as evidence of that concept's salience, we can construct time-series of intellectual attention that trace the arc of ideas: slow gestation in specialist subfields, explosive diffusion into mainstream discourse, eventual routinization, and, in some cases, displacement by successor concepts.

The computational turn in the social sciences has amplified both the ambition and the feasibility of such approaches. Lazer et al.'s (2009) call for a "computational social science" argued that large-scale digital trace data — including publication metadata — could enable discovery of regularities invisible to traditional qualitative methods.[22] Salganik's synthesis of digital social research methods further established that observational data drawn from online systems, including open scholarly databases, constitute a legitimate and powerful empirical resource.[23] The open-science movement has been decisive here: the shift from subscription-walled citation indices to freely accessible infrastructure such as OpenAlex has democratised bibliometric analysis in ways that were structurally impossible a decade ago.[19,20]

This paper documents the design, data pipeline, and empirical findings of Social Science Word Map — an open-access interactive visualization built for the web. The tool covers 98 canonical social-science terms across seven subfields (Sociology, Political Science, Economics, Psychology, Anthropology, Communication, and Methods), spanning 1970–2024 with real publication data and 2025–2054 with logistic projections. It is designed to be accessible to researchers, students, journalists, and policy audiences who want an evidence-based picture of where social-science attention has been and where it is plausibly headed. The Gulbenkian Commission's (1996) call for open social science — for knowledge that crosses disciplinary boundaries and speaks to publics beyond the academy — animates the design philosophy throughout.[36]

2   Theoretical Framework

2.1   Scientometrics and Bibliometrics

The quantitative study of science has a long and distinguished history. Price's Little Science, Big Science (1963) identified exponential growth in scientific literature as a structural feature of modern knowledge production, and proposed that the doubling time of publication volume (~15 years at mid-century) was a fundamental constant of organised science.[1] Garfield's development of the Science Citation Index (1955) transformed the citation into a quantifiable trace of intellectual influence,[2] and Broadus's (1987) systematic survey of the term "bibliometrics" mapped the field's terminological consolidation.[3]

The field subsequently extended its methods from journal-level to author-level and concept-level analysis. Hirsch's h-index (2005) proposed a single scalar measure of individual scientific impact that proved both practically useful and theoretically contentious.[4] Small's (1973) co-citation analysis established that clusters of co-cited papers identify invisible colleges — communities of researchers working on the same intellectual frontier before formal subdisciplines crystallise.[5] Tools such as CiteSpace (Chen, 2006)[6] and VOSviewer (van Eck and Waltman, 2010)[7] operationalised these methods in widely used software, enabling knowledge-mapping at the scale of entire disciplines.

More recent work has shifted attention to what Fortunato et al. (2018) call "the science of science" — a programme that uses large-scale publication and citation data to study universal regularities in how knowledge is produced, credited, and diffused.[9] Waltman's (2016) comprehensive review of citation impact indicators provides the methodological rigour that underpins such work, clarifying the conditions under which bibliometric measures are and are not valid proxies for intellectual influence.[10] The present project situates itself within this tradition, applying term-frequency analysis at the concept level across a 55-year longitudinal window.

2.2   Knowledge Field Theory

Bourdieu's (1975) theory of the scientific field provides a sociological counterpoint to purely quantitative bibliometrics. In Bourdieu's account, disciplines are structured social spaces in which agents compete for symbolic capital — the authority to define what counts as legitimate knowledge.[11] Terms that gain wide adoption represent not only intellectual advances but also political victories within these fields: the rise of intersectionality in sociology, for example, reflects decades of sustained advocacy by feminist scholars of colour alongside genuine analytical utility. Bibliometric trajectories are, on this reading, partial records of field-level power struggles.

Collins's (1998) The Sociology of Philosophies demonstrates at encyclopaedic scale that intellectual movements require social infrastructure — chains of interaction ritual, networks of teachers and students, institutional niches — to sustain conceptual momentum.[12] Rapid growth in a term's doc-frequency share typically corresponds to the consolidation of such a community: journals launch, conferences specialise, training programmes incorporate the new vocabulary. Abbott's (2001) analysis of the ecology of academic disciplines further shows that disciplines compete for intellectual jurisdiction over problems, and that the success of a new concept often depends on its capacity to colonise adjacent fields.[13]

2.3   Discourse as Data

Foucault's (1969) The Archaeology of Knowledge argues that discourses are not simply collections of statements but structured systems that determine what can be said, by whom, and in what institutional context.[16] Van Dijk's (1997) programme of critical discourse analysis extended this framework to systematic empirical analysis, treating texts as social practices that reproduce or contest ideological structures.[17] Bibliometric methods provide a quantitative complement to these qualitative approaches: rather than close reading of canonical texts, we aggregate the statistical footprint of concepts across entire corpora, detecting the moments at which discourses crystallize (rapid growth), plateau (stable share), or fragment (declining share).

The two approaches are not in competition — bibliometrics identifies the when and how fast; discourse analysis illuminates the why and with what power effects. Lamont and Molnár's (2002) synthesis of boundary-work studies demonstrates how social scientists themselves draw and contest the symbolic boundaries that determine which concepts count as rigorous and which as ideological — a process our data traces at the aggregate level but cannot decompose at the level of individual boundary disputes.[18]

2.4   The Sociology of Intellectual Movements

Kuhn's (1962) account of scientific revolutions identified the paradigm as the shared framework of concepts, methods, and exemplars that defines a scientific community's "normal science."[15] Paradigm shifts — moments at which a new framework displaces an established one — are visible in our data as sharp discontinuities: the rapid growth of poststructuralism in the late 1970s and intersectionality in the 2000s both exhibit the inflection-point pattern Kuhn describes. Pre-paradigmatic terms show high variance and low share; during consolidation, share grows steeply; post-consolidation, growth decelerates as the term becomes taken-for-granted background.

Frickel and Gross's (2005) general theory of scientific and intellectual movements provides a more fine-grained account of how new conceptual frameworks mobilise, recruit, and institutionalise.[14] Their model emphasises the role of "intellectual opportunity structures" — gaps in existing frameworks that new concepts can occupy — and "organisational contexts" — the conferences, journals, and funding bodies that can sustain or suppress a movement. The acceleration of emergence documented in Section 8.4 is consistent with the hypothesis that digital communication has lowered the barriers to intellectual movement formation, compressing the mobilisation phase that Frickel and Gross document as typically spanning a decade or more.

3   Contributions to the Literature

Existing bibliometric dashboards — including Web of Science's analytics module, Scopus's trend tools, Dimensions, and the Lens.org platform — typically require institutional subscriptions or present results at the level of journals, authors, or citation networks rather than individual conceptual terms tracked longitudinally. Tools like VOSviewer[7] and CiteSpace[6] produce rich knowledge maps but require specialist expertise, local data exports, and desktop software — barriers that exclude the majority of potential users. Waltman's (2016) review of indicator validity further notes that most commercial platforms embed normalisation choices that are opaque to non-specialist users.[10]

Social Science Word Map contributes the following to this landscape: (1) a freely accessible, interactive web interface requiring no installation, login, or institutional affiliation; (2) a curated cross-disciplinary term vocabulary spanning seven subfields with transparent selection criteria; (3) a 55-year longitudinal window grounded in real publication data from an open, reproducible source; (4) a 30-year forward projection using logistic modelling, making future discourse trajectories exploratively visible; and (5) a design optimized for non-specialist audiences, embedding contextual metrics (share, growth, novelty) directly in the visualization without requiring the user to understand the underlying algebra.

The broader open-science movement provides the enabling context for these contributions. Piwowar et al.'s (2018) comprehensive audit of open-access publishing demonstrated that a majority of recent scientific literature is now freely readable online,[20] and Priem et al.'s (2022) launch of OpenAlex established free, API-accessible, comprehensive bibliographic infrastructure that makes large-scale bibliometrics reproducible by anyone with a Python script.[19] This project is a direct beneficiary of that infrastructure shift.

4   Data & Sources

4.1   OpenAlex

All publication counts are drawn from OpenAlex (Priem et al., 2022),[19] a free and open catalog of scholarly works maintained by OurResearch. As of 2026, OpenAlex indexes over 250 million works, 243 million authors, and 20,000 venues, making it the largest open-access bibliographic database available. Unlike Scopus or Web of Science — both of which require institutional subscriptions and impose rate limits on data exports — OpenAlex is fully accessible via a public REST API without authentication, enabling reproducible, large-scale bibliometric queries at low marginal cost.

Queries are scoped to works tagged with the OpenAlex concept Social sciences (C17744445) — a hierarchical concept derived from the Microsoft Academic Graph taxonomy and applied to works via an automated machine-learning classifier. This scoping is imperfect (the classifier occasionally mis-tags or omits works at disciplinary margins, particularly in interdisciplinary fields such as environmental studies or science and technology studies) but provides a principled and consistent definition of the social-science corpus across all 85 years of the dataset. The classifier's consistency over time is a more important property than its absolute accuracy for the purposes of longitudinal trend analysis.

4.2   Term Selection

The 98 terms were selected through an iterative process anchored in four criteria: (a) disciplinary canonicity (the term appears prominently in major textbooks or handbooks of the subfield, such as the Handbook of Sociological Theory or the Annual Review of Political Science); (b) temporal variation (the term is documented as having risen or fallen significantly since 1970 in existing historiographic or bibliometric literature); (c) searchability (the term is sufficiently distinctive to yield a meaningful signal in title-and-abstract search, with disambiguation applied where necessary — e.g., "social class" rather than "class," "labor market" rather than "labor"); and (d) cross-subfield balance, targeting approximately 11–18 terms per subfield to prevent any single discipline from dominating the visualization.

4.3   Scope & Coverage

The real-data window runs from 1970 to 2024. Coverage before 1990 is materially sparser: OpenAlex's indexing of social-science journals improves steadily as digitisation programmes expanded through the 1990s, and many pre-1985 journal volumes exist in OpenAlex only through retrospective scanning projects of varying completeness. The 1970 start date was chosen for conceptual rather than purely statistical reasons — it marks the approximate crystallization of the modern social-science vocabulary captured in this dataset, with many tracked terms (intersectionality, neoliberalism, postcolonial) either absent or marginal before that decade. Users should interpret pre-1990 data as directionally informative but subject to significant downward count bias that may overstate the apparent growth of terms in the 1990s.

The baseline for doc-frequency share computation — total social-science papers per year — grew from approximately 98,771 papers in 1970 to 1,777,647 in 2020, a roughly 18-fold expansion over five decades. This growth reflects three partially separable phenomena: genuine expansion in global social-science research capacity (more researchers, more institutions, more journals); the progressive digitisation of older literature into OpenAlex; and the reclassification of previously non-indexed venues as open access mandates spread. Because our metric is share rather than raw count, all three phenomena are controlled for in the primary analyses.

4.4   Reproducibility and Open Data

Full reproducibility is a design requirement of this project. The complete data pipeline — from raw OpenAlex API calls through metric computation to the final JSON payload — is implemented in a single Python script (fetch_openalex.py) available in the project repository. An API response cache (openalex_cache.json) stores all query results so that the pipeline can be re-run without exhausting API quota; only terms with missing or incomplete cache entries trigger new API calls. The output files (words.json and the equivalent data.js wrapper) are committed to the public GitHub repository alongside the visualization code, making the complete dataset — not merely summary statistics — available for inspection and reuse.

Piwowar et al.'s (2018) audit of open-access scholarly literature documents the structural shift that makes this approach viable: the majority of recent scientific output is now accessible without institutional intermediaries.[20] The present project extends this logic from data consumption (reading open papers) to data production (generating open bibliometric datasets). The use of OpenAlex rather than Scopus or Web of Science was motivated partly by this principle: a methodology that requires a commercial subscription cannot be reproduced by most researchers in the world, particularly those in the Global South whose institutions lack the resources for comprehensive database licensing.

5   Methodology

5.1   Doc-Frequency Share

For each term t and year y, the doc-frequency share is defined as the fraction of all social-science papers in that year whose title or abstract contains the search query associated with t:

Doc-frequency share
s(t, y) = count(t, y) / total_SS(y)

where count(t, y) is returned by the OpenAlex works endpoint filtered by concept, publication year, and title-abstract keyword; and total_SS(y) is the analogous count without a term filter. This normalisation is essential: raw counts grow simply because total social-science output grows, as Bornmann and Mutz (2015) document across disciplines.[8] Doc-frequency share isolates the concept's salience relative to the field's overall production at each point in time, analogous to the term-frequency component of tf-idf weighting but applied at the corpus-year level rather than the document level. Unlike tf-idf, our measure does not penalise terms that appear in many documents — ubiquity is precisely what we wish to measure.

5.2   Growth (5-yr CAGR)

The five-year compound annual growth rate of doc-frequency share captures the momentum of a term's trajectory:

5-yr CAGR
g(t, y) = ( s(t, y) / s(t, y−5) )^(1/5) − 1

The five-year window is chosen to smooth year-to-year publication noise arising from conference proceedings, special issues, and funding-cycle artefacts, while remaining responsive to genuine trend shifts that typically unfold over three to seven years. Annual windows produce erratic series for low-frequency terms; ten-year windows over-smooth, masking inflection points in rapidly changing fields. Terms with a CAGR above +5%/yr are classified as rising; below −2%/yr as declining; within that band as stable. For terms emerging from near-zero share in early years, CAGR is capped at +200%/yr to prevent arithmetic distortions from small-denominator ratios.

5.3   Novelty Index

The novelty index measures what fraction of a term's total real-data mentions occurred in the most recent five years of the dataset:

Novelty Index
n(t) = Σ count(t, y) for y ∈ [2020,2024] / Σ count(t, y) for y ∈ [1970,2024]

A high novelty score (approaching 1.0) indicates a term that is essentially a product of the last five years — either a genuinely new concept (e.g., decolonial, misinformation) or a term propelled by a recent exogenous shock (covid, pandemic). A low novelty score indicates an established term whose publication history is distributed across decades rather than concentrated recently. Novelty is a static property of the term across its full longitudinal history, not year-dependent, and is displayed as a contextual metric in the detail panel alongside the year-specific growth and share values.

5.4   Validity Considerations

Title-and-abstract search is a coarse instrument for concept measurement. The central validity assumption is that a term's presence in an article's title or abstract is a reliable indicator that the concept it denotes is substantively engaged in that article — not merely mentioned in passing or used in a narrowly technical sense unrelated to the social-science meaning under investigation. This assumption holds better for highly distinctive terms (intersectionality, neoliberalism) than for polysemous ones (structure, network, identity). Moed's (2005) foundational work on citation analysis as a research evaluation tool identified analogous precision–recall trade-offs in citation-based measures; our term-frequency approach faces the same challenge at the vocabulary level rather than the citation level.[35]

A secondary validity concern involves the relationship between term frequency and conceptual influence. High doc-frequency share does not necessarily indicate that a concept is intellectually central — it may indicate that a concept has become a routine citation obligation, deployed in introductory paragraphs without doing analytical work. Citation-weighted share (weighting each paper by its forward citation count) would partially address this confound by up-weighting papers in which the concept appears to have been analytically generative. We identify this as a priority for future work; in the current dataset, raw and citation-weighted shares are likely highly correlated for most terms, since conceptually productive papers tend to be cited more heavily.

6   Projection Methodology

6.1   Logistic Growth for Rising Terms

For terms with a recent five-year CAGR exceeding +2%/yr, the projection uses a discrete logistic model. The carrying capacity K is estimated as a multiple of the 2024 share, capped to prevent implausible concentrations of scholarly attention in a single concept:

Carrying capacity
K = min( s(t, 2024) × (1 + CAGR)^20 , 8 × s(t, 2024) )
Logistic projection
s_proj(t, y) = K / ( 1 + (K/s(t,2024) − 1) × exp(−r × 0.6 × τ) )

where τ = y − 2024 and r is the estimated CAGR. The 0.6 damping factor reflects the empirical regularity that growth rates in academic discourse rarely sustain at their recent peak: as a concept matures, it becomes assumed background rather than explicit foreground, and its measured frequency plateaus even as its intellectual influence persists. The logistic form was chosen over simple exponential projection because unconstrained exponential growth implies that a single term could eventually dominate the entire corpus — an outcome inconsistent with the diversity-maintenance dynamics observed in bibliometric data.

6.2   Decay and Stability

For declining terms (CAGR below −2%/yr), the projection applies an exponential decay toward a positive floor — the share at which a term persists as a latent vocabulary even when it is no longer a growth area:

Decay floor
floor = max( s(t, 2024) × 0.05 , 0.00003 )
Decay projection
s_proj(t, y) = floor + (s(t, 2024) − floor) × exp(CAGR × τ)

The positive floor reflects the empirical observation that no concept in this dataset reaches true zero share — even concepts with sharply declining trajectories (cold war, structuralism) maintain a residual presence in review articles, intellectual histories, and specialist journals that keep the vocabulary alive in archival form. For stable terms (CAGR within ±2%/yr), the projection applies a gentle mean-reversion toward 95% of the 2024 share over the thirty-year window, reflecting the long-run drift of even stable vocabularies as the concept matures and its usage spreads from core subfields into adjacent disciplines where it may be deployed less precisely.

6.3   Baseline Projection

The total social-science publication volume for 2025–2054 is itself projected using a logistic model fitted to the 2017–2024 growth rate of the OpenAlex Social Sciences baseline, capped at 5%/yr to reflect the expected slowing of global journal expansion as open-access mandates mature, institutional budgets stabilize, and market saturation approaches in the academic publishing sector. Projected shares are computed as projected raw counts divided by this projected baseline, maintaining the normalisation methodology consistent with the real-data period. All projected years are flagged with p: 1 in the data structure and rendered with visual distinction (dashed lines, purple colouring) throughout the interface to ensure users are not misled into treating projections as historical observations.

7   Visualization Design

The visualization is implemented as a single-file HTML application using D3.js v7 for layout and rendering. This architectural choice — no build step, no server requirement, no framework dependency — ensures the tool is deployable on any static host (GitHub Pages in the current deployment) and can be opened directly from a local file system for offline use. The entire application, including all styles, interaction logic, and the embedded research paper, is contained in a single ~90KB HTML file, complemented by a ~450KB data payload (data.js) that works via both file:// protocol and standard HTTP.

Treemap layout. Block area is proportional to doc-frequency share, implementing Shneiderman's (1992) original treemap principle: the visual weight of each cell directly encodes the concept's relative prominence in the selected year.[21] The squarified layout algorithm minimises long thin rectangles, maximising the legibility of cell labels. Subfields are rendered as parent rectangles with coloured headers; terms are leaves. A double-click on a subfield header zooms into that discipline, temporarily hiding other subfields to enable within-discipline comparison.

Color layers. The visualization supports four switchable metric layers: 5-yr Growth (diverging red–grey–green scale centred at zero, where red encodes declining and green encodes rising terms), Doc-frequency share (sequential blue gradient), Novelty (sequential amber gradient), and Raw count (sequential blue gradient). The combination of cell size (always encoding share) with cell colour (encoding any of the four metrics) enables bivariate reading of the treemap, allowing users to identify, for example, large but declining terms (high share, negative growth, red fill) without additional visual channels.

Temporal dimension. The year scrubber encodes the full 85-year window from 1970 to 2054. Real-data years (1970–2024) are rendered with a solid blue fill track; the projected zone (2025–2054) is rendered with a dashed purple overlay and separated by a vertical divider at 2024. A play button animates the treemap through all 85 years at 380 ms per year — roughly 30 seconds for a full traversal — making the temporal dynamics viscerally apparent in a way that static cross-sections cannot convey.

Detail panel and search. Clicking any treemap cell opens a 300px right panel showing the term's four metrics for the selected year; a full history chart spanning 1970–2054 (solid line for real data, dashed purple for projected) with a moving year marker; the term's peak year and first-seen year; and the eight highest-share related terms in the same subfield, each clickable as a pivot. A text search field in the header filters the treemap in real time, dimming non-matching cells and enabling rapid navigation to specific terms without scrolling.

8   Empirical Findings

The findings reported here are grounded in 55 years of real publication data (1970–2024) from the OpenAlex Social Sciences corpus. Absolute count figures in parentheses refer to cumulative papers in the real-data window. All growth claims are based on five-year CAGR computed at year 2024 unless otherwise specified.

8.1   Rising Discourses

Rising — Psychology

Mental health (206,204 cumulative papers) has shown the steepest sustained growth of any Psychology-subfield term. Its trajectory tracks several overlapping historical processes: the deinstitutionalisation movement of the 1970s–80s that shifted psychiatric care from hospitals into communities and research into populations; the DSM-III's (1980) proliferation of operationalised diagnostic categories that created standardised research targets; the emergence of evidence-based medicine as a framework that required measurable psychological outcomes; and — most dramatically — the COVID-19 pandemic's acceleration of population-level mental health research from 2020 onward, which produced a near-vertical uptick in the corpus share. Twenge et al. (2020) document the secular increase in self-reported mood disorder indicators across birth cohorts that provides the sociological substrate for this bibliometric trend.[27]

Rising — Sociology

Intersectionality (118,325 papers) traces a trajectory characteristic of theoretical innovations originating at disciplinary margins. Introduced by Kimberlé Crenshaw (1989) in a legal studies context to describe how race and gender interact in ways invisible to single-axis frameworks,[24] the concept diffused slowly through the 1990s before explosive growth from approximately 2010. Cho, Crenshaw, and McCall's (2013) programmatic article mapping "a field of intersectionality studies" codified the concept's theoretical ambitions and methodological requirements,[25] and McCall's (2005) empirical typology of intercategorical, intracategorical, and anticategorical approaches provided the methodological scaffolding that made the concept tractable across disciplines.[26] The data show adoption spreading from sociology into political science, education, public health, and communication from 2012 onwards.

Rising — Methods

Machine learning (60,793 papers) and artificial intelligence (81,531 papers) display the steepest single-decade growth curves in the entire dataset, both concentrated almost entirely in the period 2017–2024. This reflects the rapid adoption of computational methods across social-science disciplines — from natural language processing applied to historical corpora and survey responses, to reinforcement-learning-based models of human decision-making, to fairness and accountability research examining algorithmic systems. Lazer et al.'s (2009) early call for a computational social science envisioned precisely this cross-disciplinary uptake,[22] and the data suggest that uptake accelerated significantly after the widespread availability of pre-trained large language models from 2017 onward.

Rising — Political Science / Economics

Neoliberalism (89,412 papers) shows a protracted rise spanning four decades — from near-zero in 1970 to a peak share in the mid-2010s — consistent with Boas and Gans-Morse's (2009) finding that the term migrated from a narrow technical description of a specific policy programme to a broad critical descriptor applied across political science, economics, sociology, and geography.[32] Harvey's (2005) influential account of neoliberalism as a class project provided the widely-cited theoretical synthesis that accelerated cross-disciplinary diffusion from the mid-2000s.[31] The data show neoliberalism's growth decelerating after 2016, consistent with the term becoming sufficiently ubiquitous to function as background vocabulary rather than analytical foreground.

Rising — Communication / Methods

Climate change (188,027 papers) and social media (142,650 papers) represent the two largest absolute-count rising terms in the dataset. Climate change's growth within the social-science corpus — distinct from, but entangled with, its natural-science counterpart — reflects the field's engagement with climate governance, environmental justice, climate communication, and the socioeconomic dimensions of energy transition. Carvalho's (2007) analysis of how ideological cultures mediate media coverage of climate science documents the discursive dynamics that our bibliometric data captures in aggregate.[37] Social media's trajectory is even steeper, essentially nonexistent before 2007 and accounting for a substantial fraction of Communication-subfield papers by 2020.

8.2   Declining Discourses

Declining — Political Science

Cold war (109,805 papers) shows the sharpest historically legible decline in the dataset: high and rising through the 1980s as Cold War tensions, area studies programmes, and superpower competition dominated political science and sociology, the share drops steeply after 1989 and continues to fall through the 2010s as the bipolar geopolitical framework recedes from active research vocabulary. The post-2022 uptick following Russia's invasion of Ukraine is visible in the data as a minor inflection — an increase in the raw count — but not a reversal in share terms, as the overall social-science corpus has grown so substantially that a modest absolute increase in "cold war" mentions barely registers as a share movement.

Declining — Sociology / Anthropology

Structuralism (424,809 papers — high cumulative total, but steadily declining share) peaked in the 1975–1985 period when Lévi-Straussian analysis, Althusserian Marxism, and structural-functionalist sociology simultaneously dominated anthropology, sociology, and literary studies. Its gradual displacement by poststructuralism, cultural studies, and practice theory — frameworks that emphasised agency, contingency, and power in place of synchronic deep structures — is visible in the share data as a slow but consistent decline from the mid-1980s onward. The parallel rise of habitus, practice, and discourse in the same period traces the conceptual succession at the vocabulary level.

Declining — Communication / Political Science

Propaganda (68,397 papers) has declined as an active research vocabulary even as the phenomena it originally described — state and non-state manipulation of information environments — have intensified dramatically. Its conceptual successor terms, misinformation and disinformation, show explosive post-2015 growth. Vosoughi, Roy, and Aral's (2018) landmark Science paper demonstrating that false news spreads faster and farther than true news on Twitter[29] crystallised the misinformation research programme and contributed directly to the sharp acceleration visible in the data after 2018. The substitution of "misinformation" for "propaganda" illustrates how disciplines rename persistent phenomena to accommodate new theoretical frameworks and new empirical contexts, rather than simply abandoning the underlying questions.[30]

8.3   Emergent Terms

Emergent — Anthropology / Communication

Decolonial (13,640 papers) and misinformation (23,217 papers) represent terms that were essentially absent from the corpus before 2013 and have since grown at rates among the highest in the dataset. Decolonial thought — building on Quijano's (2000) concept of the coloniality of power[33] and Mignolo's (2007) theorisation of colonial difference and epistemic justice[34] — spread from Latin American philosophy and postcolonial studies into anthropology, education, development studies, and museum studies from approximately 2015 onward. The timing corresponds to a broader political moment — the Rhodes Must Fall movement (2015), the Standing Rock protests (2016), and the intensification of decolonisation debates in university curricula — that created institutional demand for the vocabulary.

Emergent — Psychology / Management

Burnout (31,732 papers) and mindfulness (22,196 papers) emerged as significant research terms in the 2010s, both reflecting the intersection of occupational health psychology with organisational management research. Burnout's trajectory, theorised originally by Maslach and Leiter (1997, revised 2016) as a syndrome of exhaustion, cynicism, and reduced efficacy in human-service work,[28] accelerated substantially during the COVID-19 pandemic as healthcare worker burnout became a public-health crisis subject to systematic study. Mindfulness's growth tracks the integration of contemplative practice into evidence-based cognitive therapies and, subsequently, into corporate wellness programmes — a diffusion from clinical psychology into management, education, and positive psychology that our data traces from approximately 2012.

Emergent — Political Science / Sociology

Populism has shown exceptional growth since 2015, driven by the simultaneous emergence of populist political movements across Europe, the Americas, and South Asia that created immediate demand for conceptual frameworks. The term's bibliometric trajectory illustrates a characteristic feature of politically reactive social science: a real-world shock generates a surge in research publications that precedes, rather than follows, theoretical consensus — much of the early post-2016 literature uses "populism" as a descriptive label whose analytical content remains contested.

8.4   Cross-Subfield Patterns

Several structural patterns emerge from a cross-subfield reading of the data. First, the psychologisation of social problems: concepts previously theorised primarily at the structural level — inequality, poverty, precarity, discrimination — increasingly appear alongside psychological vocabulary (trauma, wellbeing, burnout, resilience) in the same papers. This reflects the disciplinary expansion of psychology into domains historically occupied by sociology and political economy, and the corresponding demand from policy audiences for individual-level interventions that are more tractable than structural reform. The parallel growth of mental health and inequality in the corpus, without a corresponding growth of papers that integrate both frameworks, suggests that psychologisation often proceeds alongside rather than in dialogue with structural analysis.

Second, the digital turn's uneven disciplinary diffusion: digital and computational terms (algorithm, social media, machine learning, big data, computational methods) have grown most rapidly in Communication and Methods, at moderate rates in Economics and Political Science, and significantly more slowly in Anthropology and Sociology. This heterogeneity reflects differences in methodological tradition, training infrastructure, and epistemic culture. Salganik's (2018) framework for digital social research documents the theoretical and practical barriers that slow adoption in qualitatively oriented disciplines,[23] while Lazer et al.'s (2009) original manifesto explicitly identified the institutional resistance of established social-science departments as a constraint on the computational turn.[22]

Third, the temporal compression of conceptual emergence: comparing the arc of neoliberalism (roughly 20 years from coinage to mainstream prominence, 1975–1995) with misinformation (mainstream in under five years, 2015–2020) or decolonial (similar speed) suggests that concepts now diffuse through the social-science corpus substantially faster than they did in the pre-internet era. Frickel and Gross's (2005) model of intellectual movement formation emphasises the role of networks and communication channels in mobilisation speed;[14] the acceleration visible in our data is consistent with the hypothesis that social media, open-access publishing, and preprint servers have collectively lowered barriers to cross-disciplinary concept diffusion in ways that are structurally new.

Fourth, crisis-driven episodic spikes: several terms show sharp departures from their underlying trends in response to geopolitical or public-health shocks. The 9/11 attacks produced a measurable but transient spike in terrorism and security; the 2008 financial crisis drove a multi-year elevation in financial crisis, austerity, and inequality; and the COVID-19 pandemic produced the most dramatic episodic spike in the dataset, temporarily making pandemic and covid among the highest-growth terms by any measure. In each case, growth decelerated as the immediate shock receded, though the long-run share often stabilised at a permanently elevated level — consistent with Kuhn's observation that crises can accelerate conceptual change without fully resolving into new paradigms.[15]

9   Limitations

Anglophone and journal bias. OpenAlex's social sciences corpus is weighted heavily toward English-language, journal-based scholarship. Moed's (2005) systematic analysis of bibliometric databases demonstrates that non-English-language journals are consistently under-indexed relative to their true scholarly output,[35] and social-science publication norms vary substantially across national contexts — French sociology, German sociology, and Latin American sociology each maintain significant publication traditions in national languages and in book formats that are poorly captured by title-and-abstract search of journal articles. Results should be interpreted as reflecting predominantly Anglophone, journal-based social science, with the caveat that this is itself not a neutral sample: Anglophone publication norms have become increasingly dominant globally as journal metrics penetrate non-Anglophone academic systems, making the corpus's biases partly self-reinforcing.

Classifier consistency and coverage gaps. The OpenAlex Social Sciences concept tag (C17744445) relies on an automated machine-learning classifier derived from the Microsoft Academic Graph. Classifier behaviour may have changed across the full 55-year window as the underlying model was updated, and retroactive classification of pre-1990 papers is necessarily less reliable than classification of recent papers that were present in the training data. Inter-disciplinary journals, book chapters, theses, and working papers are less reliably indexed than articles in established social-science journals. The pre-1990 period is most affected: users should treat absolute share values before 1990 as approximate and focus on direction rather than level.

Term ambiguity and search validity. Title-and-abstract keyword search maps imperfectly onto conceptual presence. The term network retrieves papers on social network analysis, computer networks, policy networks, and actor-network theory — substantially different intellectual projects. Disambiguation queries applied during term selection reduce but cannot eliminate polysemy noise. For the most ambiguous terms in the dataset, the trend direction is more interpretable than the absolute share level. This limitation is most severe for terms drawn from the Methods subfield (regression, experiment) where the word appears routinely in papers that are not about the method as an object of study.

Projection uncertainty. The logistic extrapolation to 2054 is a statistical model fit to past trends, not a forecast. It cannot anticipate genuinely new concepts, paradigm shifts in the Kuhnian sense,[15] major disciplinary crises, or exogenous shocks (wars, pandemics, technological discontinuities) that historically produce the largest departures from trend lines. The further the projection extends from the data anchor (2024), the wider its implicit confidence interval. Our projections should be read specifically as "what the recent trend implies if it continues at a damped logistic rate" — a conditional statement about the inertia of existing trajectories, not a prediction about what social science will in fact study in 2054.

Selection and curation effects. The 98 terms constitute a curated sample, not an exhaustive inventory of social-science vocabulary. Terms absent from the dataset include important concepts in area studies, science and technology studies, legal sociology, economic geography, and interdisciplinary fields such as global health and urban studies. The selection reflects the judgement of a single researcher working from a specific disciplinary vantage point in 2025–2026, and carries the corresponding blind spots. Future versions should incorporate structured term elicitation from domain experts across the seven subfields to reduce individual curation bias.

10   Future Research Directions

Real-time updating. Scheduled OpenAlex API calls — run monthly via a simple CI/CD pipeline — would keep the dataset current without manual intervention. The existing cache architecture already supports incremental updates: only years with new data need re-fetching. A versioned data release system would allow users to track how the current-year snapshot changes over time, adding a meta-bibliometric dimension to the tool.

Citation-weighted share. Weighting each paper's contribution to term frequency by its forward citation count would distinguish widely-cited conceptual contributions from lower-impact deployments of the same vocabulary. This would partially address the validity concern identified in Section 5.4 and would likely sharpen the signal for terms that are analytically central to a field versus those that are routinely mentioned without being analytically engaged. OpenAlex provides forward citation counts via the same API, making this extension technically straightforward.

Co-occurrence network analysis. A co-occurrence view — showing which terms in the dataset tend to appear in the same papers — would reveal the conceptual neighbourhoods and interdependencies invisible in the current term-by-term analysis. Terms that consistently co-occur form intellectual clusters; changes in co-occurrence patterns over time trace shifts in the conceptual architecture of social science. Small's (1973) original co-citation methodology[5] provides the theoretical foundation; the OpenAlex API provides the data infrastructure.

Multilingual extension. Extending the dataset to include French, German, Spanish, and Portuguese social-science corpora — all partially indexed by OpenAlex — would address the Anglophone bias documented in Section 9 and enable comparative bibliometrics across linguistic traditions. The Gulbenkian Commission's (1996) critique of Anglophone hegemony in social science[36] provides the normative motivation; the methodological challenge is the development of cross-linguistically equivalent search terms that track the same concepts across linguistic contexts where they may carry different theoretical connotations.

LLM-assisted term scoring. An interactive feature allowing users to enter any research concept — including terms not in the current dataset — and receive an AI-generated analysis of its bibliometric trajectory, disciplinary positioning, and theoretical genealogy would substantially extend the tool's utility for exploratory research. This feature, analogous to the "Score a Job" capability in the companion KarmaMap project, would combine the existing quantitative data infrastructure with the contextual knowledge embedded in large language models, producing a hybrid human-AI interface for conceptual discovery.

Conclusion

Social science is not a fixed body of knowledge but a living conversation, shaped by the political urgencies, institutional incentives, and intellectual fashions of each era. The Social Science Word Map makes this conversation visible — not as a static snapshot but as an 85-year film, from the structuralist and behaviouralist debates of the 1970s, through the cultural and poststructural turns of the 1980s and 1990s, the computational turn of the 2000s, the identity and crisis scholarship of the 2010s, and the projected futures of the 2040s and 2050s. Each frame of that film corresponds to a real year in the OpenAlex corpus; the transitions between frames encode the pace of conceptual change.

The empirical findings confirm and quantify what intellectual historians have documented through close reading: the rise of identity-centred frameworks in sociology and anthropology; the psychological reframing of social problems; the computational turn in methods; the explosive emergence of concepts tied to digital media, misinformation, and climate change; and the gradual retreat of Cold War geopolitical vocabulary and structuralist analytical frameworks. The visualization adds a dimension that qualitative intellectual history cannot easily provide: relative pace. The data show that misinformation achieved in five years what neoliberalism took twenty — a quantitative observation with significant implications for how scholars understand the acceleration of conceptual change in the digital era.

The dataset, code, methodology, and visualization are fully open and freely accessible. We invite researchers to build on this foundation: correcting the term list, extending the subfields, improving the projection model, integrating richer data sources, or applying the same methodology to other disciplinary corpora. The tool documents a process — the collective self-organisation of social-science attention — that is ongoing. Every paper published tomorrow will shift, by a small increment, the share values this tool displays. The map is never finished, because the territory it traces is still being made.
